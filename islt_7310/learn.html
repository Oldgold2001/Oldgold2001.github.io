Learning Goals

Goals

Learning Goal 1 - Students will understand the concept of Judicial Review.

Learning Goal 2 - Students will be able to identify the issue being decided in 11 important US Supreme Court cases.

Learning Goal 3 - Students will match the 11 Landmark US Supreme Court Cases with the Constitutional Amendment they are based on.

Learning Goal 1

Marbury v. Madison

Judicial Review is a process that is not officially defined in the US Constitution. However, it is an important part of the checks and balance system we have in our government. Through Judicial Review the courts can check the power of the Legislative and Executive branch of the United States government. By making decisions on questions brought before the Supreme Court they can declare a law or act to be unconstitutional or further clarify the interpretation of how the law should be applied to the citizens of the country. Judicial Review was originally set up in the court case Marbury v. Madison in 1803.

Learning Goal 2

11 Landmark Cases

There are many court cases that are decided on every year. For the purposes of this website, we will focus on 11 Landmark US Supreme Court cases selected by the American Bar Association. Students will learn about the issue of the case and what caused it to be heard by the Supreme Court. This is often called the question of the case. Students will learn about the basic facts of the case as well as the decision. Next the students will go to the oyez.com website to research the cases further. By looking at this website students will be able to see the majority, concurring, or dissenting opinion for each of the 11 cases.

American Bar Association Article

Court Cases

Case names will link to information on www.oyez.org/
  • Marbury v Madison (1803) - Even though case itself was not as important it established Judicial Review.
  • McCulloch v Maryland (1819) – Could the state of Maryland pass laws that interfered with the National Bank established by Congress? Established implied powers for Congress and federal supremacy.
  • Gibbons v Ogden (1824) – Gave the federal government more power to regulate the economy.
  • Dred Scott v Sanford (1857) – Stated Slaves were property and helped lead to Civil War.
  • Brown v Board of Education (1954) – Overturned segregation laws in schools and laid the ground work for the Civil Rights movement of the 60s.
  • Gideon v Wainwright (1963) – Established the defendants right to a lawyer in all state criminal trials.
  • Miranda v Arizona (1966) – Said rights must be told to anyone being accused of a crime by the police. Created the Miranda Rights.
  • Tinker v Des Moines (1969) – Set parameters to First Amendment freedom of speech rights for students in schools.
  • Roe v Wade (1973) – Central case dealing with abortion rights in the U.S.
  • Regents of the University of California v Bakke (1978) – Important case that up affirmative action programs as constitutional.

Learning Goal 3

How do the Justices decide?

The next step in understanding the court cases is looking at the amendments they are based upon. The question of each case is based off an amendment or article of the US Constitution. The Supreme Court justices will look at the Constitution and decide based on how they interpret the Constitution. By matching the Landmark Court Cases up with the appropriate article or amendment it will allow the students to see the connection between the case and the Constitution.

US Constitution

Connections to the US Constitution


How does each case connect to the US Constitution? The table below lists the court case in Column 1. Column 2 cites the part of the Constitution the Justices used. The final column explains the reasoning the Court used to explain their decision.
Court Case Constitution Citation Reasoning of Court
Marbury v Madison Article III Section 2
Article VI Paragraph 2		 The Court said the Judiciary Act of 1789 was unconstitutional. The reason was Supremacy Clause said the Constitution was above laws made by Congress.
McCulloch v Maryland Article I Section 8 Set up the power of the Elastic Clause saying that it allowed Congress to make laws that were related or “Necessary” to executing enumerated powers in the Constitution.
Gibbons v Ogden Article I Section 8
Article VI Paragraph 2 		Justices cited the Commerce Clause and the Supremacy Clause. First, the Commerce Clause gave Congress the ability to regulate interstate trade. Second, the Supremacy Clause said the national laws would override the laws of the state.
Dred Scott v Sanford 5th Amendment The Courts stated that slaves were considered property and according the 5th amendment and the government could not take property away from US citizens.
Schenck v U.S. Article I, Section 8, Clause 11
1st Amendment 		The war powers of Congress gave them the ability to enact the Espionage Act. The Court further cited Freedom of Speech in the 1st Amendment did not protect speech that can cause a “clear and present” danger to citizens.
Brown v Board of Education 14th Amendment The policy of “Separate but Equal” was established in an earlier case. Brown v Board found segregated schools were not constitutional according the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th amendment.
Gideon v Wainwright 6th Amendment
14th Amendment 		The 14th Amendment extended rights given in the US Constitution to the state level. The 6th amendment right to counsel was a right of defendants in both Federal and State trials. As a result, states must provide a lawyer for anyone who cannot afford one.
Miranda v Arizona 5th Amendment In a close decision the Court said according to the 5th amendment all people being questioned by the police should be told their Constitutional Rights. Dissenting opinions believed this was an over extension of the 5th amendment rights and that it took power away from the police to question suspects.
Tinker v Des Moines 1st Amendment The Justices cited the 1st Amendment right to freedom of speech. They stated that students did not lose their right to freedom of speech or expression when they came into school. However, it was noted this freedom only extends to speech that does not interfere with the operation of the school.
Roe v Wade 9th Amendment
14th Amendment 		The 9th Amendment gives citizens rights that are not directly mentioned in the Constitution. One of these rights is the right to privacy. The Court believed that laws limiting abortions violated this right since pregnancy was considered a personal issue. The 14th Amendments incorporation clause extended the 9th Amendment liberties to the states.
Regents of the University of California v Bakke 14th Amendment The Court stated racial quota systems used for college admissions were not constitutional according to the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment. The decision however was confusing because there was not a single majority opinion. In fact, one of the justices who voted in the majority actually agreed with both opinions and as a result gave court support of affirmative action initiatives.
